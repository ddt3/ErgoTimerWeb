## About me, the developer
## A stroke I didn't see coming

On Wednesday morning, June 25, 2025, my life changed in an instant. I had a stroke — at 55. It wasn't on my bingo card, as I later wrote on my blog. One day I was a software developer going about my work; the next I was lying in the Brain Care Unit at VieCuri hospital, left-sided paralysis, trying to type with a hand that didn't cooperate.

I was lucky. On Thursday I walked. On Friday I could climb stairs and go home. Physically, things moved faster than I had any right to expect.

## Recovery from the inside

What no one tells you about recovering from a stroke — especially when your physical recovery is relatively quick — is how brutally exhausting your brain becomes. Cognitive fatigue is invisible and relentless. Planning a single day suddenly required more mental energy than I had.

I started looking for a tool that could help me structure my time, balance mental tasks with rest, and remind me to take micro-pauses before I hit the wall. I couldn't find one that felt right. So I built it myself.

## Why ErgoTimer

ErgoTimer was born out of necessity. As a software developer with a technical background, I had the skills to build what I needed — even if building it was, in itself, part of my rehabilitation. The app reflects everything I learned from my own recovery: that structure reduces cognitive load, that rest is not optional, and that a calm, uncluttered interface matters when your brain is already working overtime.

ErgoTimer is designed from the inside out — by someone who lives with Acquired Brain Injury every day.
